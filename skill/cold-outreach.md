# Cold Outreach to Grant Reviewers

A warm application beats a cold one every time. Grant reviewers are human — they fund people they've heard of, people who've been helpful in Discord, people who show up at events. This file covers how to build that familiarity before you apply.

---

## Why outreach matters

Most founders submit an application and wait. The ones who get funded have usually:
- Been visible in the program's Discord for weeks before applying
- Replied helpfully to questions in their area of expertise
- Attended at least one IRL event where program leads were present
- DM'd the right person once with something genuinely useful — not an ask

None of this is manipulation. It's how ecosystems work. People fund people they know.

---

## The right order

**Wrong order:** Apply → Get rejected → Try to connect with the reviewer → Ask why

**Right order:** Connect → Be helpful → Apply → Follow up → Get funded

Start relationship-building 4-6 weeks before you plan to apply.

## How to build visibility before applying

**In Discord (3-4 weeks before applying):**
- Answer questions in your area of expertise — even one good answer per week
- Share relevant resources when someone asks for them
- Post about what you're building in #show-and-tell or equivalent channels
- Don't pitch your project unprompted — just be helpful

**On X (ongoing):**
- Reply to posts from program accounts with genuine insight
- Tag them when you ship something relevant
- Quote-tweet announcements with your own take — not just "great news!"

**At IRL events:**
- Introduce yourself to leads in person — one sentence on what you're building
- Follow up with a DM after: "Great to meet you at [event]. I'm building [X] — would love to show you a demo sometime."

## The cold DM formula

If you haven't met someone in person, a cold DM can still work — if it's not an ask.

**Structure:**
1. One sentence establishing who you are and why you're relevant
2. One sentence of something genuinely useful (a resource, an observation, a connection)
3. No ask — or a very soft one at the end

**Example (Superteam regional lead):**
```
Hey [Name], I run Web3 Mumbai — we've had 3,000+ members come through 
our events over the last year.

Noticed you're looking for regional builders for [initiative] — happy 
to connect you with some strong Mumbai devs if useful.

Also working on [project] and planning to apply for a Superteam grant 
next month. Would love to get your eyes on it when it's ready.
```

**Example (Foundation grants team):**
```
Hey [Name], I've been building [project] on Solana for the last 3 months — 
it solves [specific problem] for [specific user].

Came across your post about [relevant topic] and thought this thread 
might be relevant to what you're thinking about: [link to your own content]

Planning to apply for a Foundation grant next month. If you have 15 mins 
for a quick call beforehand, I'd value your input on the application.
```

**What makes a good cold DM:**
- Specific — they can tell you actually know who they are
- Gives before asking — a resource, a connection, a useful observation
- Short — under 5 sentences
- One soft ask maximum — not "can you guarantee my grant will be approved"

**What kills a cold DM:**
- Starting with "I hope this message finds you well"
- Asking for funding in the first message
- More than 3 paragraphs
- Generic flattery ("Love what you're doing at Superteam!")

---

## Following up after submitting

After you submit an application, one follow-up is acceptable. Two is too many.

**Timing:** 10-14 days after submission if you haven't heard anything

**Format:**
```
Hi [Name],

Following up on my [Program] application for [Project Name] submitted 
on [date].

Since submitting, I've [specific update: shipped M1, added 50 users, 
won a Superteam bounty]. Happy to share any additional context.

[Your name]
```

Keep it short. One update. No pressure.

---

## What not to do

- Don't DM multiple people at the same program with the same message
- Don't ask someone to "put in a good word" unless you have a real relationship
- Don't pitch in public channels unprompted ("anyone know who to talk to about grants?")
- Don't follow up more than twice total — one after submission, one if you get silence past 3 weeks

---

## The relationship that compounds

The founders who get funded multiple times aren't the ones with the best single application. They're the ones who treated every interaction with the ecosystem as a relationship investment — showed up at events, helped people, shipped publicly, and made grant reviewers feel like funding them was an obvious call.

Build that reputation before you need the grant.
